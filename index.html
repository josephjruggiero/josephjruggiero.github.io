Working papers

Bargaining, War, and Cooperation in the Long Run

Preventive War with Sovereign Debt (with Colin Krainin, Kris Ramsay, Bella Wang)

R&R at Conflict Management and Peace Science

Abstract

The preventive motive for war arises because states cannot commit to limit the use of their growing power to shape the future distribution of material benefits and there are not enough resources available today to compensate the declining state for their expected losses tomorrow. In this article, we model how international capital markets affect preventive war incentives by introducing a profit-maximizing bond market to the canonical bargaining model of war. We find that, with borrowing, preventive wars remain possible. The nature of the power shift and fundamentals of the market for sovereign debt interact to determine when a preventive motive is more likely to lead to preventive war. Two main results show that (1) potentially large, but uncertain, power shifts are most dangerous and (2) unlike the direct effect of interest rates on the cost of war, higher interest on sovereign debt makes preventive war more likely. We find evidence for the latter effect by extending Lemke's (2003) study of preventive war for major-power dyads between 1816 and 1992.
